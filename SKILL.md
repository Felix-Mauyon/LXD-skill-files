=== HOW TO USE THE EXAMPLES BELOW ===

The three examples that follow are real emails David sent. They are reference material for VOICE, TONE, and the KIND of moves David makes — not templates to fill in. Study them for:
- How he opens (always a quick, casual, specific question — never the same phrasing twice)
- How he introduces himself (varies: sometimes a "Quick context" bullet list, sometimes a flowing paragraph — vary it yourself)
- How he transitions into the prospect's world (always specific and varied in wording)
- How he frames his two proof points (sometimes as statements, sometimes as questions inviting dialogue — see Pat's email, where they're framed as "How do orgs..." / "How are you approaching...")
- How he pivots to a close (NEVER the same sentence twice — "You named the real gap yourself" is ONE example of ONE move he made ONCE with Melissa specifically because she'd used that exact phrasing publicly. Do not reuse this sentence verbatim for other contacts. If pivot_quote is present, write an ORIGINAL sentence that naturally references it, modeled on the spirit of these examples, not their exact wording.)
- His PS style: always personal, always specific, always different in structure

Do NOT copy phrases verbatim from these examples into a new email unless the underlying fact is also true for this contact (e.g. only mention NYC/Knicks if this contact has an NYC connection). Generate fresh sentences that sound like the same person wrote them, using different words each time.

=== EMAIL FORMAT (elements that should appear, in roughly this order — vary the language and structure naturally, the way the three examples vary from each other) ===

1. SUBJECT LINE: 6-12 words. Name a specific tension, gap, or opportunity in this contact's world — real tools, companies, frameworks, or concepts from their dossier. Never generic ("Connecting," "Following up," "Quick question").

2. OPENER: "Hi [first name],\nDavid here." then a short, casual, specific hook — a conference question, a shared post, a direct ask to connect. Keep it brief (1-2 sentences). Vary the phrasing every time — do not reuse "Open to trading notes" or any other fixed phrase verbatim across emails.

3. INTRO/CONTEXT: Introduce David briefly using facts from the CREDENTIALS block above, in your own words. This can be 2 short bullets (Example 1, 2 style) OR a flowing paragraph (Example 3 style) — choose whichever fits the email's rhythm better. Don't always default to bullets.

4. BODY: 1-2 sentences connecting to the prospect's specific situation (from prospect_detail and angle_description). Real names, dates, specifics — never generic.

5. TWO POINTS: Present David's two relevant proof points (from chosen_proof_points) using the PROOF POINT FACTS above, in fresh language. These can be framed as statements or as open questions inviting the contact's perspective (see Example 3). Number them, label each with a short word (Measurement / Engagement / Assessment, etc. as fits).

6. PIVOT/CLOSE: One original sentence connecting everything to a soft ask to talk. If pivot_quote is non-empty AND genuinely self-authored, you may echo it back — but write a NEW sentence each time; do not reuse "You named the real gap yourself" as a fixed phrase. If pivot_quote is empty, connect the prospect's situation to David's offering without implying they said anything.

7. SIGN-OFF: "Best from Atlanta,\nDavid Fu\nhttps://www.linkedin.com/in/davidthefu/" — optionally followed by 1-3 short title lines (see Example 3) when it adds useful context; not required every time.

8. PS: exactly one PS — 1-2 sentences. Use ps_detail as-is: a genuine, specific two-way connection between something in David's life (his network, places he's lived, things he's built or written) and something true in the contact's world. This can lead with David's own story (e.g. a networking anecdote, a shared place, a shared framework) OR with a personal detail about the contact — whichever is more specific and genuinely connects the two. Do not force it to start with the contact; match the pattern shown in the example emails, where the PS is whatever creates the most real, concrete connection.

TONE: direct, plain-spoken, no jargon, conversational. Read the three examples again before writing — they should sound like the same person, but no two should read like they came from a template.

LENGTH — HARD LIMIT: Look at the three examples. Match their length. The body (everything between the opener and the sign-off, excluding subject and PS) should be under 200 words. Write tight. Cut any sentence that doesn't add a new fact or move the email forward. If your draft is over 200 words, cut the INTRO/CONTEXT section first, then tighten BODY to one sentence.

Output format — output ONLY this, no preamble, no commentary:
SUBJECT: [subject line here]

[email body here]
